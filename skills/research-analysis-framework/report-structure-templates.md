# Report Structure Templates - Professional Document Architecture

## Purpose

Provide reusable templates for organizing findings into decision-ready professional reports. Different situations require different structures; use templates as starting point and adapt to context.

---

## Template 1: Standard Analysis Report (12-20 pages)

### Structure

```
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1 page - READ THIS FIRST)
2. DETAILED FINDINGS (8-12 pages - THE ANALYSIS)
3. EVIDENCE & SOURCES (2-3 pages - THE PROOF)
4. RECOMMENDATIONS (1-2 pages - WHAT TO DO)
5. APPENDICES (varies - SUPPORTING DETAIL)
```

---

### SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1 page)

**Purpose**: Decision-maker has 5 minutes. Give them the essentials.

**Content**:
```
ANALYSIS TITLE
[Clear problem/decision being addressed]

THE QUESTION
"Should we proceed with [decision]?"

KEY FINDING (One sentence)
[Your bottom-line conclusion]

VIABILITY SCORE (if applicable)
[0-10 score or PROCEED/CAUTION/STOP]

RECOMMENDATION
[What should the decision-maker do?]

TOP 3 RISKS
1. [Risk with impact]
2. [Risk with impact]
3. [Risk with impact]

NEXT STEPS
1. [Action 1]
2. [Action 2]
3. [Action 3]

TIMELINE
Decision deadline: [date]
Expected implementation: [date]

APPENDIX REFERENCE
"See Detailed Findings for evidence"
```

**Example**:
```
HOUSING INVESTMENT DECISION

The Question: Should we invest R$ 550k in building a house for personal residence?

Key Finding: Project is VIABLE with proper risk management, but financial buffer is 
inadequate as currently budgeted.

Recommendation: PROCEED IF: Budget is increased to R$ 575k (to include 15% contingency)
Or REDUCE SCOPE to fit R$ 500k budget

Viability Score: 7.3/10 (Good but with concerns)

Top 3 Risks:
1. Budget has zero contingency (could break project if costs rise)
2. Material cost inflation risk not adequately buffered
3. Contractor change mid-project would be expensive

Next Steps:
1. Approve revised budget of R$ 575k with contingency
2. Lock in supplier quotes immediately
3. Confirm contractor availability for 12-month timeline

Expected Implementation: 12-14 months from permit approval
Decision Deadline: March 31, 2024
```

### SECTION 2: DETAILED FINDINGS (8-12 pages)

**Purpose**: Reader understands the analysis in depth. How did we reach conclusions?

**For Viability Assessment, use this structure**:

```
[For Each Dimension: Technical, Financial, Regulatory, Market, Operational, Risk]

DIMENSION: TECHNICAL FEASIBILITY
────────────────────────────────────

Assessment Approach
├─ What we evaluated: (building codes, structural design, material standards)
├─ How we evaluated: (document review, expert consultation, comparison)
└─ Sources used: (ABNT standards, structural engineer review, building inspection)

Key Findings
├─ Finding 1: [Specific statement of fact with evidence base]
├─ Finding 2: [Another finding]
└─ Finding 3: [Related finding]

Evidence Quality
├─ Source Tiers: TIER 1 sources (codes), TIER 2 (project examples)
├─ Confidence: 🟢 HIGH (95%+) - Official standards are authoritative
└─ Gaps: None in technical feasibility assessment

Risks in This Dimension
├─ Risk: Soil conditions could require foundation redesign
├─ Probability: MEDIUM (geotechnical testing is recommended)
├─ Impact: 3-4 week delay if redesign needed
└─ Mitigation: Conduct soil testing now; design certified before construction

Mitigations Proposed
└─ Geotechnical testing → Foundation design certification → Proceed

TECHNICAL SCORE: 8/10
Rationale: Technically sound, standard residential construction, proven methods.
Minor risk around soil conditions that is manageable with testing.
```

**Repeat structure for each dimension, then add**:

```
CROSS-DIMENSIONAL INSIGHTS
────────────────────────────────────

Where Dimensions Intersect
├─ Financial tightness (budget 0% contingency) directly impacts Risk management
├─ Market strength makes project viable even with financial constraints
├─ Operational execution (contractor quality) is critical given budget pressure
└─ Technical simplicity helps offset financial/operational uncertainty

Cumulative Risk
If multiple dimensions have issues, compound risk increases:
├─ Example: "Tight budget + inexperienced contractor = HIGH RISK"
└─ Current situation: "Tight budget but excellent contractor = MANAGEABLE"

Overall Viability Profile
"Project has strong foundation (market, technical, regulatory)
but is constrained by tight financial position requiring either:
(1) Budget increase with contingency, or
(2) Scope reduction"
```

### SECTION 3: EVIDENCE & SOURCES (2-3 pages)

**Purpose**: Reader can verify findings or dig deeper.

**Content**:

#### 3.1 Source Inventory

```
Source | Tier | Topic | Use | Limitations
─────────────────────────────────────────────
ABNT NBR 6118 | 1 | Building codes | Regulatory baseline | Generic; local variation
CREA 2024 Report | 2 | Labor costs | Cost benchmarking | Aggregate data
5 Project Cases | 2 | Timeline realness | Validation | Local market only
Contractor Quotes | 3 | Material costs | Data point | Specific to their network
Analyst Reports | 3 | Market trends | Context | Opinions, not data
```

#### 3.2 Chain of Reasoning

```
Question: "What's the realistic timeline?"

Tier 1 Source (Weight: 40%)
ABNT Standards specify standard cure time = 28 days
└─ Leads to: Minimum 6 months for structural completion

Tier 2 Source (Weight: 35%)
+ Five completed local projects = 11-13 months typically
+ Activity-based calculation = 12 months
└─ Leads to: 12 months is realistic for full project

Tier 3 Source (Weight: 25%)
+ Contractor estimate = 12 months with standard crew
+ Market commentary = no major supply chain bottlenecks
└─ Converges to: 12-month timeline

Conclusion: 12 months is realistic baseline; 13 months with 1-month buffer recommended
```

#### 3.3 Contradictions Found & Resolved

```
Contradiction 1:
├─ Source A says: Material costs are stable (industry commentary)
├─ Source B says: Inflation emerging (analyst report)
├─ Assessment: Source B is TIER 2 (established analyst), Source A is TIER 3 (commentary)
├─ Resolution: Honor Source B concern; include contingency
└─ Action: Built in 12% contingency for potential inflation

Contradiction 2:
├─ Contractor says: Can do in 11 months (aggressive)
├─ Historical data says: 12 months typical (realistic)
├─ Assessment: Contractor understating (optimism bias likely)
├─ Resolution: Use 12-month baseline + 1-month contingency = 13 months
└─ Action: Recommend 13-month timeline; contractor to confirm feasibility
```

#### 3.4 Confidence Matrix

```
Finding | Confidence | Basis
───────────────────────────────────────────
Building codes apply | 🟢 HIGH (98%) | ABNT official standard
Permit timeline 3-4 wks | 🟡 HIGH (85%) | Municipal data + project history
Foundation cost R$ 25-35/m² | 🟡 MEDIUM (75%) | Benchmarks + quotes
Total timeline 12 months | 🟡 MEDIUM (80%) | Standards + projects + contractor
Labor shortage emerging | 🟠 LOW (60%) | Contractor anecdotes (not verified)
Material prices stable | 🔴 UNVERIFIED | Assumption (no current data)
```

#### 3.5 Gaps in Available Information

```
Critical Information NOT Available
├─ Actual soil conditions (geotechnical report pending)
├─ Exact material supplier quotes (received preliminary only)
└─ Regulatory approval timeline (not submitted yet)

Impact Assessment
├─ Soil conditions: Could impact foundation design (3-4 week risk)
├─ Material quotes: Could affect budget (±10% risk)
└─ Regulatory timeline: Could delay start (1-month risk)

Recommended Actions
├─ Complete soil testing before permitting
├─ Lock supplier quotes before breaking ground
└─ Submit permits immediately to establish timeline
```

### SECTION 4: RECOMMENDATIONS & NEXT STEPS (1-2 pages)

**Purpose**: What should reader do now?

**Content**:

#### 4.1 Major Recommendations

```
RECOMMENDATION 1: Increase Budget to R$ 575k
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Rationale: Current budget (R$ 500k) has zero contingency; industry standard is 15%
Current Situation: Estimated cost is R$ 550k with no buffer
Impact: If even minor overrun occurs, project breaks financially
Action: Increase authorized budget to R$ 575k (R$ 550k cost + R$ 25k contingency)
Owner: Finance approval required
Timeline: Approve before construction begins
```

#### 4.2 Sequenced Actions (What to do, in order)

```
IMMEDIATE (Next 2 weeks)
├─ [ ] Approve revised budget of R$ 575k
├─ [ ] Complete geotechnical testing
└─ [ ] Submit building permit application

NEAR-TERM (Month 1-2)
├─ [ ] Lock supplier quotes for all major materials
├─ [ ] Confirm contractor availability & crew composition
├─ [ ] Receive permit approval
└─ [ ] "Notice to Proceed" issued

EXECUTION (Months 3-14)
├─ [ ] Foundation phase (months 1-2)
├─ [ ] Structural phase (months 3-6)
├─ [ ] MEP phase (months 5-10)
├─ [ ] Finishes phase (months 9-12)
└─ [ ] Final inspection & occupancy (month 13-14)
```

#### 4.3 Decision Criteria

```
GO/NO-GO DECISION

PROCEED IF:
├─ Budget approved at R$ 575k ✓
├─ Geotechnical testing shows no surprises ✓
├─ Contractor confirms 12-month timeline ✓
└─ Permits on track for Q1 approval ✓

CAUTION IF:
├─ Geotechnical testing reveals poor soil conditions
├─ Contractor indicates timeline will be 14+ months
├─ Permit process indicates delays

STOP IF:
├─ Budget cannot be increased beyond R$ 500k
├─ Contractor expresses serious concerns about feasibility
├─ Zoning or regulatory barriers emerge
```

#### 4.4 Contingency Plans

```
SCENARIO 1: Foundation redesign required (geotechnical issue)
├─ Cost impact: +R$ 20-40k
├─ Timeline impact: +3-4 weeks
├─ Mitigation: Contingency budget covers cost; schedule buffer absorbs delay
└─ Action: Escalate to sponsor; proceed with mitigation plan

SCENARIO 2: Material cost inflation >10%
├─ Cost impact: +R$ 55k (at 10% inflation on R$ 550k)
├─ Timeline impact: Possible delays if supply chain affected
├─ Mitigation: Contingency covers up to 10% inflation
└─ Action: Monitor; escalate if exceeds contingency

SCENARIO 3: Contractor becomes unavailable
├─ Cost impact: Possible higher rates for replacement
├─ Timeline impact: 2-4 week delay for procurement + mobilization
├─ Mitigation: Have backup contractor list; negotiate rates now
└─ Action: Quick procurement; absorb delay in schedule buffer
```

#### 4.5 Monitoring Plan

```
WHAT TO TRACK | HOW OFTEN | THRESHOLD | ACTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Budget spent vs. planned | Weekly | ±10% variance | Escalate if exceeded
Timeline (milestones) | Weekly | ±3 days | Escalate if behind
Quality (inspections) | Daily/weekly | Zero major defects | Rework immediately
Contractor performance | Bi-weekly | On-track metric | Review if slipping
Material costs | Monthly | Lock quotes early | Negotiate extensions
Supplier delivery | As needed | On-time | Expedite if needed
```

### SECTION 5: APPENDICES

**Include**:

```
A. Detailed Cost Breakdown (spreadsheet format)
B. Detailed Timeline/Gantt Chart
C. Source Documents (copies of key references)
D. Contractor Quotes & References
E. Geotechnical Report Summary (when available)
F. Permit Requirements Checklist
G. Full Assumptions List
H. Calculation Workings (complex formulas explained)
```

## Template 2: Quick Decision Brief (1-2 pages)

For when decision-maker has only 5 minutes total.

```
DECISION REQUIRED: [What's being decided?]

ANSWER: [Yes/No/Conditional]

WHY: [One sentence explanation]

IF YES:
├─ Do this first: [Priority action]
├─ Timeline: [When]
└─ Owner: [Who]

IF RISKS:
├─ Risk 1: [description + impact]
├─ Risk 2: [description + impact]
└─ Mitigations: [How you're managing risks]

QUESTIONS? See full report attached. Key sections: [reference]
```

## Writing Standards for Reports

✅ **DO**:
- Lead with finding (not methodology)
- Use clear headings & white space
- Back claims with sources
- Mark confidence levels
- Include caveats & limitations
- Make document skimmable
- Provide executive summary
- Explain jargon

❌ **DON'T**:
- Bury recommendations in text
- Use passive voice (own conclusions)
- Claim certainty you don't have
- Hide contradictions
- Make document too long (12-20 pages ideal)
- Use academic tone (be clear)
- Assume reader knows context
